Problem

It can be difficult to manage and track your income when it mainly consists of tips and tends to fluctuate on a weekly, if not daily basis.







Solution

A straight-forward and intuitive app with a suite of features that allows users the ability to configure tips, track multiple jobs/shifts, view income reports, and set reminders to input tips.

User Surveys

I conducted a user survey in order to better understand the user and their motivations. I distributed the survey amongst my peers and gathered 22 responses.

View User Survey

I split the participants into two groups for questioning based on their tip tracking habits.

group a

Those who didn’t or sometimes did keep track of tips:

Why don’t you track your tips?
• Laziness
• Forgetfulness
• Opting to keep track mentally

60% of participants never considered using an app.

What kind of features and qualities would need to be added for you to use a tip tracking app?
• Simple and user friendly
• Separate inputs for cash and credit card tips
• Weekly, bi-weekly, and monthly totals
• Reminders or notifications





group b

Those who did keep track of tips on a regular basis:

Other methods included keeping money in a designated drawer for counting at the end of each week and tracking on a notebook.

88.9% of participants utilize their method of choice because it is easy. As this isn’t a collaborative or social app, it’s not a surprise that influences from coworkers/peers did not affect their methods.





group a+b

I collectively asked both groups what they would like to track and configure. Additionally, I asked participants to rank features in terms of importance.

Important features:
• Push notifications for reminders
• Track multiple shifts per day
• Export data

Competitive Analysis

I performed a competitive analysis on apps from the survey: TipSee and Just the Tips. As well as Google Sheets since spreadsheets were used just as equally.

PROS
• Ability to add notes
• Quickly input tips
• Auto/Manual Backup


CONS
• Advanced tip tracking features need to be unlocked with ads or a subscription plan.
• Poor visual design
• Doesn’t look credible

PROS
• Extensive customizable features for tip tracking
• Online Backups
• No ads or fees


CONS
• Signup process requires email verification before accessing the app
• Users report frequent glitches and freezes
• Customization features aren’t intuitive

PROS
• Credible and reliable
• Automatically saves to the clouds
• Fully customizable


CONS
• Templates for tracking your tips are not readily available
• High learning curve if the user isn’t familiar with spreadsheets
• Lacks features specifically for tip tracking

key takeaways:

I was able to identify standard features and design patterns that users are accustomed to when tracking tips or data. The apps designed to track tips included all of the key features mentioned in the surveys however they weren’t intuitive to use and had dated visual designs which lead to a lack of credibility. I was honestly shocked at how poorly designed these apps were and they're somehow highly rated in the app markets.

Google Sheets, on the other hand, was very easy to use but wasn’t specifically catered for a tipped worker.

Sketches + Low Fidelity Wireframes

With the user stories and flows completed, I had a better idea of how to structure my wireframes based on hierarchy. I created and tested several rounds of paper prototypes before creating digital wireframes.

Establishing the wireframes was the most challenging phase during this process. It was difficult to implement customizable tip options for every job, position, and shift in a way that was intuitive for the user.

View sketches + wireframes
paper prototype 1

The first design based on my content strategy and site map completely failed. During my first usability test, a few participants didn’t understand why there were such extensive customizable features. Many struggled with how the “Jobs” page was laid out and had difficulty understanding what they were customizing. Additionally, a few mentioned these features were typically found under “Settings” and it felt unnatural in a place other than “User/Account”.

To clarify, I initially wanted users to have the ability to input their Job (employer) and their position(s) (bartender, server, busboy, etc). In most cases, they are all tipped differently and I wanted users to have the ability to customize their data and/or notifications. Even while sketching out concepts, I was frustrated with how I could lay the information out in a way that was easy for the user to understand. I couldn’t draw inspiration from similar apps for design patterns as they weren’t intuitive either.

I later realized that the idea of extensive customization came from TipSee and Just the Tips. The research gathered showed no indication that this was a necessary feature.

View Paper Prototype 1
paper prototype 2

After my first usability test, I spent some time looking through design patterns and features in different apps. I was able to draw inspiration from Google Drive, Otter, Headspace and Uber .

Using the feedback gathered from before, I worked on simplifying the product altogether, adding an account setup, and getting rid of the extensive customizable options. I was heavily inspired by Uber and implemented swiping the bottom drawer up for advanced options.

While testing the second prototype, I changed tasks slightly according to the revisions:

1. Create an account.
2. Add a detailed tip, meaning one that includes both cash and credit card.
3. Create a new job for your account.
4. View a detailed income report from 02/09 - 02/15.
5. Export your data.

Participants successfully completed all five tasks and I was able to move my designs to Figma.

Brand + Identity

After looking at the current App Market, I found that many successful, popular products had short and memorable names. Many of them opted to combine words or create a name using a play on words. After a few rounds of preference tests, I finalized the name to Tippo.

As my target audience ranged primarily from 18 to 35, I wanted the logo to evoke a sense of both playfulness and reliability.

colors

I went with an analogous color palette using shades of blue and a pop of bright green. The product primarily uses a dark, navy blue to evoke a sense of reliability and strength. The bright pop green serves to bring an energizing and vibrant energy to the otherwise plain UI.

typography

In order to keep things clean, I decided to utilize only one font, Roboto, and work with different font weights. As the app is going to display a lot of numbers, I looked for font faces that were tabular (monospaced).

High Fidelity Mockups

After establishing my product’s brand and curating a style guide, I was able to work on designing my high-fidelity mockup screens.

It took me a couple of rounds of iteration and feedback before finalizing the mockups. The changes made in the first and second rounds were implemented after a senior designer looked over my screens and suggested revisions in order to further leverage the visuals. The revisions made on the third round were implemented after running usability tests.

revisions round 1

Feedback from a senior designer

1. Changed colors on buttons to better indicate they were disabled.
2. Added the signature green color to other sections of the app to add cohesion to the screens and to also guide users towards important features/tasks

In addition to the color changes, I took out some of the line dividers on the side menu to add dimension to the page.

revisions round 2

Feedback from a senior designer

1. Added red to indicate a destructive action for the “Delete Job” button.
2. Added labels to text inputs as opposed to having placeholders.

revisions round 3

Usability Testing

I assigned participants of the usability tests with the same tasks used to create my second round of paper prototypes:

1. Create an account.
2. Add a detailed tip, meaning one that includes both cash and credit card.
3. Create a new job for your account.
4. View a detailed income report from 02/09 - 02/15.
5. Export your data.

When tasked with creating a new job, several participants initially clicked on the “Add” tab on the bottom navigation. After further exploration, they would successfully complete the task on the side menu.

Additionally, participants were confused with the option to “Enable” a job. Again, I was drawing inspiration from the competitors’ apps because I found that a majority of them had that feature. Admittedly, I wasn’t sure of its exact purpose even after further research. I could only make assumptions.

Due to the confusion, I got rid of the ambiguous feature and implemented the “Primary Job” option for autofill purposes when adding a tip. I added in an explanation under the box for further clarification.

Revisions:
1. Added a drop-down menu for jobs on all of the tip input fields
2. Removed the “Enabled” switch button on the “Create Job” screen and changed it to a checkbox option to indicate a primary job, meaning which job is auto filled when trying to input a tip.

revisions round 3

Usability Testing
(Continued)

When tasked with viewing a detailed income report, participants pointed out that it was difficult to differentiate the headings for the graphs, customizable time periods when viewing reports would be beneficial, and the green color on the segmented controls were confusing as it usually indicated clickability/action.

Additionally, all participants tried to export data from the Reports tab.

Revisions:
1. As opposed to three different sections for Total Income in the Reports tab, I opted for only one graph at a time with customizable options for the time period.
2. Changed the color on the segmented controls to the blue accent color.
3. Implemented an additional method of exporting data in the Reports tab.

Conclusion

As I have worked a tipped position for the last ten years, I figured I had first hand experience on what was needed to create this product. It only took one round of usability testing for me to realize how wrong I was.

I heavily relied on my usability testing results when creating different iterations of the product as there weren’t many similar apps to draw design inspiration from. In fact, the features I attempted to emulate from competitor apps all failed during my tests, in particular the extensive customizable options.

Much like my other projects, this was a very humbling experience and I have learned how important usability testing can be.
